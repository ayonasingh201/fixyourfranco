Common Beginner Difficulties

Click each topic to learn more and get tips to overcome it.

Hard

French has many silent letters — the final consonant of a word is usually not pronounced. For example, vous sounds like "voo," and beaucoup sounds like "bo-koo." The letter H is almost always silent too.

💡 Tip: Listen to native speakers on YouTube or podcasts daily. Apps like Forvo let you hear how each word is pronounced by real French speakers.
Tricky

Every French noun is either masculine (le/un) or feminine (la/une), and there is no strict rule — you must memorize each noun's gender. For example, le livre (the book) is masculine, while la table (the table) is feminine.

💡 Tip: Always learn the article with the noun. Instead of memorizing "table," learn "la table" as one unit. Color-code your notes: blue for masculine, pink/red for feminine.
Complex

French verbs change depending on the subject (je, tu, il/elle, nous, vous, ils/elles) and tense. There are regular verbs (-er, -ir, -re) and many irregular verbs like être (to be) and avoir (to have) that must be memorized separately.

💡 Tip: Master the present tense first. Use conjugation tables and practice with apps like Anki. Learn the most common irregular verbs (être, avoir, aller, faire) as a priority.
Moderate

French uses accents that change pronunciation and meaning. The main ones are: é (acute), è/ê (grave/circumflex), ç (cedilla), and ô/â. Forgetting an accent can change the meaning of a word — ou means "or," while où means "where."

💡 Tip: Change your phone or keyboard language to French. This gives you quick access to accented characters and makes writing practice much easier.
Cultural

French has two ways to say "you": tu (informal, for friends and family) and vous (formal, for strangers, elders, or professional settings). Using the wrong one can seem rude or overly casual.

💡 Tip: When in doubt, always use vous. French people will often invite you to use tu (called "tutoyer") when it's appropriate.
Intermediate

Liaison is when the normally silent final consonant of a word is pronounced when the next word starts with a vowel. Example: les enfants sounds like "lay-zan-fahn." Elision is when a vowel is dropped before another vowel — je aime becomes j'aime.

💡 Tip: Don't panic — liaisons become natural with listening practice. Focus on speaking and the patterns will come automatically over time.
Confusing

French numbers can be baffling: 70 is soixante-dix (sixty-ten), 80 is quatre-vingts (four-twenties), and 90 is quatre-vingt-dix (four-twenty-ten). This requires a different way of thinking about numbers.

💡 Tip: Practice counting out loud every day — count steps, count items in the fridge, count anything! Make it a daily habit and it will become second nature.
Pronunciation

French has nasal vowels that don't exist in English: an/en, in/ain, on, and un. The sound comes from letting air flow through the nose. Words like bon (good), vin (wine), and pain (bread) use nasal sounds.

💡 Tip: Watch your mouth in a mirror while practicing. Try humming softly while saying these sounds — the vibration in your nose is the key!

Study Tips for Success

Practical habits that accelerate your French learning journey.

🎧

Listen Every Day

Even 15 minutes of French music, podcasts, or YouTube trains your ear to the rhythm and sounds of the language.

🗣️

Speak Out Loud

Don't just read — say words and sentences aloud. Talk to yourself in French. Pronunciation improves only through speaking.

📒

Use Flashcards

Apps like Anki use spaced repetition so you review words just before you forget them — scientifically proven to work.

🎬

Watch French Films

Start with French subtitles, not English. Shows like Lupin or Call My Agent are beginner-friendly and fun.

📅

Be Consistent

20 minutes every day beats 3 hours once a week. Consistency is more important than intensity. Build a habit.

🤝

Find a Language Partner

Practice with a native speaker on apps like Tandem or HelloTalk. Real conversations accelerate learning rapidly.
